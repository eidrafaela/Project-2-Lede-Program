Brazil Paid 82% More Per Kilogram for Liver-Based Products in 2025

Despite calls for a foie gras ban since 2020 through Congress, trade data shows the French product remains on the market

By: Rafaela Eid · Publish date: 12 July 2026

As Brazil moves closer to banning foie gras through the Bill 90/2020 after six years of political debate, trade data tells a different story. Despite growing pressure from animal welfare groups and legislation now awaiting President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva's decision, imports linked to the French product have continued to reach the Brazilian market — in 2025, Brazil paid the highest price per kilogram recorded in the past seven years.

Foie gras, literally "fat liver" in French, is produced through a process known as gavage. Two or three times a day, a handler inserts a metal or plastic tube down a duck or goose's throat, forcing far more food into the animal than it would consume naturally. The practice has long been condemned by animal welfare organizations, which argue it causes severe suffering.

In Brazil, that debate has moved beyond public campaigns and into Congress. In 2020, the organization Animal Equality Brasil launched a movement against foie gras production and consumption in the country, bringing renewed attention to the practice and contributing to a political push for regulation. That same year, Senator Eduardo Girão (Podemos-CE) introduced Bill 90/2020, a proposal that aims to ban the production and sale of food obtained through forced animal feeding, including foie gras.

In 2026, on April 28, the Constitution and Justice Committee approved Bill 90/2020, allowing the legislation to advance toward presidential review. The bill now awaits a decision from President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva. The bill's journey through Congress, however, has been slow.

  • 2020 Senator Eduardo Girão (Podemos-CE) introduces Bill 90/2020, proposing a ban on food obtained through forced animal feeding, including foie gras.
  • 2022 Approved by the Senate under a conclusive review process; sent to the Chamber of Deputies and assigned to three committees: Consumer Protection (CDC), Environment (CMADS), and Constitution and Justice (CCJC). The CDC rapporteur issues a favorable opinion in June, but the end of the legislative term in January 2023 forces the process to restart.
  • 2023 Regains momentum: approved by the Consumer Protection Committee in August and the Environment Committee in November, before moving to the CCJC for a vote on its legal validity.
  • 2024 Setback in April: CCJC rapporteur Tião Medeiros (PP-PR) argues the bill is unconstitutional and legally flawed. The opinion is returned to him for review; the project stalls for the rest of the year.
  • 2025 New rapporteur Fred Costa (PRD-MG) takes over and, in May, issues a favorable opinion declaring the bill constitutional, legally valid, and properly drafted, clearing it for a final committee vote.
  • 2026 On April 28, the Constitution and Justice Committee approves Bill 90/2020, sending it toward presidential review.

To investigate whether the controversy translated into a measurable impact on imports, this report analyzed official trade data from Brazil and France, examining commercial flows between 2019 and 2025.

The first analysis used Brazil's official trade database Comex Stat, searching for imports from France under the two Harmonized System Code (HS Code) codes that specifically classify foie gras: 02074300 (fatty duck livers) and 02075300 (fatty goose livers). Neither code returned any records during the period analyzed.

The absence of data under these classifications does not necessarily mean that foie gras was not imported into Brazil. Instead, it suggests that the product may have entered the country under a broader customs category, a common challenge when tracking specific food products through international trade databases.

Using HS code 16022000, which covers prepared or preserved liver products from any animal, Comex Stat returned consistent records of imports from France. Between 2019 and 2025, Brazil imported more than US$100,000 (FOB) worth of liver-based food preparations from the country.

Because HS 16022000 includes products beyond foie gras, it is not possible to conclude that every shipment classified under this code corresponded exclusively to the delicacy. However, France remains the world's leading foie gras producer and Brazil's main supplier, making this category the closest available proxy in official Brazilian customs data.

The numbers show little evidence that the political debate immediately reduced imports. In fact, the highest import value recorded during the period occurred in 2021, after Animal Equality's campaign began in Brazil. That year, Brazil imported US$31,109 FOB in liver preparations from France.

After 2021, total import values declined. However, when the data is adjusted by weight, a different trend emerges. After reaching its lowest point in 2021, when Brazil paid US$12.90 per kilogram, the average price per kilogram increased, reaching US$23.52 in 2025, an 82% rise. This was the highest unit value recorded during the period analyzed. In other words, although Brazil imported less liver-based food in both total value and volume than during previous peaks, the product reached its highest price per kilogram in 2025.

The investigation also examined French export data through the UN Comtrade Database, covering France's shipments to Brazil from 2019 to 2024, since 2025 data was not yet available. The analysis used the same HS codes applied in Comex Stat: 02074300, 02075300, and 16022000.

Again, the specific foie gras classifications provided limited results. The goose foie gras code (HS 02075300) returned only one record: a shipment valued at US$35 in 2024. Most trade activity appeared under HS 16022000, the broader category for prepared or preserved liver products.

A comparison between Brazilian import records and French export records reveals significant discrepancies between the two databases. The differences are particularly visible in unit values. In 2023, for example, Comex Stat recorded an average import price of US$21.76 per kilogram, while UN Comtrade reported US$43.73 per kilogram for French exports to Brazil, a value 101% higher than the one recorded in Brazilian import data.

These differences highlight the challenges of measuring a niche market such as foie gras through customs databases alone. Variations in classification methods, reporting practices, and trade records can influence how the product appears in international statistics.

To further investigate French exports, this report also analyzed Eurostat data on France's exports to Brazil across six HS codes: 020743, 020753, 16022010, 16022011, 160220, and 16022019. Among these classifications, only HS 16022010 ("preparations of goose or duck liver") and HS 160220 ("preparations of liver of any animal") returned available data for the 2019–2025 period.

The comparison between the two categories reveals that both classifications reported identical export values in five of the seven years analyzed: 2020, 2022, 2023, 2024, and 2025. This overlap suggests that most French exports to Brazil recorded under the broader liver preparation category correspond to foie gras products.

The only differences appeared in 2019 and 2021. In those years, the broader HS 160220 category recorded additional exports of €3,800 and €29,263, respectively, compared with the more specific foie gras category. The largest gap occurred in 2021 — the same year Brazil recorded its highest import value and UN Comtrade identified the peak in French exports to the country. The difference likely reflects other liver preparations classified outside the foie gras-specific category.

Additional figures from FranceAgriMer, France's National Institute for Agricultural and Seafood Products, provide a more specific view of foie gras trade. Unlike customs databases, which group foie gras with broader liver preparations, FranceAgriMer tracks foie gras exports directly. The agency reported that Brazil imported between 20 and 100 tonnes of French foie gras in 2019. Shipments declined during 2020 and 2021, fell to zero in 2022, and resumed in 2023 and 2024, remaining between 2 and 20 tonnes. Data for 2025 has not yet been released.

Taken together, the different datasets point to a similar conclusion: while foie gras imports have fluctuated in volume, there is no evidence that Brazil's political debate over a possible ban has halted the country's trade with the French market.

If Bill 90/2020 is signed into law, the production and sale of foie gras will be banned nationwide. As the legislation advanced through Congress, the French foie gras trade group CIFOG , Interprofessional Committee on Foie Gras Palmipeds, lobbied the Brazilian federal government against the measure, according to reports by Brazilian outlets including Veja and O Estado de S. Paulo.
